01 — Challenge

Egypt's Energy Crisis
at the Valley of Kings

Al-Qurna village in Luxor Governorate, situated near the Valley of the Kings, relies on two fossil fuel diesel generators (150 kW and 160 kW) to power lighting for its ancient tombs. This dependency creates a chain of interconnected problems threatening both the environment and Egypt's cultural heritage.

The generators produce 390 kg of CO₂ — equivalent to 106.3 kg of carbon — contributing to Egypt's already critical emissions level of 242.342 million tons of CO₂ in 2023. Constant vibrations risk structural damage to ancient tombs, while fluctuating diesel prices make the current solution economically unsustainable at ~EGP 27,000/month in energy costs.

Fortunately, the Valley of Kings has an average wind speed of 7.6 m/s — ideal for wind energy harvesting. This project was born to turn that untapped resource into clean, quiet, heritage-safe electricity.

🏛️ Monument Damage

Generator vibrations cause micro-fractures in ancient tomb rock structures, accelerating long-term deterioration of irreplaceable heritage.

💨 Carbon Emissions

390 kg of CO₂ output per operational cycle. Egypt ranks 12th globally for air pollution — diesel generators worsen an already critical situation.

💸 Economic Burden

~EGP 27,000/month in electricity costs with oil price volatility making long-term budgeting impossible for the site management.

🌡️ Noise & Heat Pollution

Generator noise disrupts the visitor experience, while exhaust heat contributes to localized temperature rise around the monuments.

02 — Innovation

The Fibonacci
Wind Turbine

🌀

Fibonacci Spiral Blades

Blades shaped by the golden ratio promote smoother airflow transitions, reduce turbulence and flow separation, and improve aerodynamic efficiency — inspired by nature's most efficient growth geometry.

📡

Flanged Wind Diffuser

A cone-shaped diffuser with a flange (h/d = 0.25) generates a low-pressure vortex behind the turbine, pulling additional airflow through the rotor and amplifying effective wind velocity to ~9.26 m/s.

🧭

Self-Orienting Tail Vane

An aluminum tail vane automatically aligns the turbine assembly with incoming wind direction via a 20 mm ball bearing, maximizing energy capture from variable desert winds.

⚙️

Gear-Coupled DC Generator

A 1:2 gear ratio (laser-cut acrylic gears) steps up rotor speed to drive a brushed DC motor as a generator, converting mechanical rotation into measurable electrical output.

♻️

Recycled Materials

The wooden shaft was salvaged from school FabLab waste. The stand was repurposed from a broken irrigation tripod. Acrylic gears were cut from offcuts — zero procurement cost for key structural parts.

📐

Compact & Portable

Total prototype volume of 0.129 m³ — well within the 0.25 m³ design requirement. The complete assembly (H: 48 cm × L: 81.25 cm) is lightweight, scalable, and easy to install on rooftops or in open spaces.

03 — Process

From Concept
to Prototype

Step 01 — Design

SolidWorks 3D Modelling

A precise 3D CAD model was built in SolidWorks replicating Fibonacci spiral geometry. The model was then 3D-printed to serve as a forming mold for aluminum blade shaping — ensuring exact 60° rotation angles and consistent blade curvature across all three blades.

Step 02 — CFD Simulation

Aerodynamic Validation

Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulations verified the design before physical fabrication. Calculated torque of 0.017 N·m and angular velocity of 209 rad/s yielded a power coefficient (Cₚ) of 44.5% — significantly approaching Betz's theoretical maximum of 59.3%.

Step 03 — Fabrication

FabLab Construction

Aluminum sheets (0.8 mm thick) were cut into 17 cm radius semicircles, shaped around the 3D mold, and fixed to the recycled wooden shaft with epoxy. The diffuser skeleton was assembled from bent metallic rods and an aluminum sheet cone. All fab tools were school FabLab equipment only.

Step 04 — Iteration

Negative Results & Corrections

Initial blades incorrectly formed an Archimedes spiral. After redesign they properly followed the Fibonacci sequence. One gear was also recalculated and re-cut after an initial dimension error caused insufficient torque transmission.

Step 05 — Testing

Dual-Trial Measurement

Two multimeters (voltage + current) were connected via breadboard and jumper wires. Trial 1 tested the bare turbine. Trial 2 added the diffuser and tail vane. Readings were captured every 30 seconds over 5 minutes under real wind conditions.
